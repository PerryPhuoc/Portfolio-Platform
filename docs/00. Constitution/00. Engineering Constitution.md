# Preamble

## Purpose

The Engineering Constitution defines the highest governing principles of the Portfolio Platform repository.

It establishes the fundamental rules, governance model, and engineering principles that regulate every technical artifact, software component, documentation asset, development process, and operational activity within this repository.

This Constitution exists to ensure long-term consistency, maintainability, scalability, traceability, and engineering quality throughout the entire product lifecycle.

---

## Authority

This document is the supreme engineering authority of the repository.

All engineering activities SHALL comply with this Constitution.

Any document, specification, source code, database object, deployment configuration, or development process that conflicts with this Constitution SHALL be considered non-compliant.

---

## Scope

This Constitution applies to every artifact maintained inside the repository, including but not limited to:

* Product Documentation
* Business Documentation
* Product Requirement Documents (PRD)
* Database Design
* Database Source Code
* Backend Source Code
* Frontend Source Code
* Architecture Documents
* Deployment Configuration
* Development Tools
* Machine-readable Specifications
* Testing Assets
* Release Documentation

No artifact is exempt unless explicitly stated.

---

## Objectives

The objectives of this Constitution are to:

1. Establish a unified engineering governance model.
2. Maintain a single and consistent interpretation of engineering rules.
3. Separate business decisions from technical implementation.
4. Preserve architectural consistency throughout the product lifecycle.
5. Prevent contradictory documentation and implementation.
6. Enable sustainable long-term development.
7. Support collaboration among contributors through standardized engineering practices.

---

## Legal Effect

This Constitution defines engineering policies rather than implementation details.

Implementation-specific documents SHALL derive their authority from this Constitution through the governance hierarchy defined herein.

Nothing in this Constitution shall prescribe specific programming languages, frameworks, libraries, databases, or tooling unless such technologies become constitutional requirements through formal amendment.

---

## Evolution

This Constitution is intended to evolve.

All modifications SHALL follow the Constitutional Amendment process defined in Chapter XII.

Historical versions SHALL remain traceable through version history and SHALL NOT be rewritten retroactively.

---

## Interpretation

When ambiguity exists, interpretation SHALL follow the intent of this Constitution rather than literal wording.

The primary objective of interpretation is to preserve engineering consistency, architectural integrity, and long-term maintainability.

No interpretation may contradict the Fundamental Principles defined in Chapter I.

# Chapter I. Fundamental Principles

## Article 1. Purpose of the Constitution

### 1.1

This Constitution establishes the fundamental engineering principles governing the Portfolio Platform repository.

### 1.2

Every engineering activity SHALL derive its legitimacy from this Constitution, either directly or indirectly through lower-level governing documents.

### 1.3

No engineering decision may contradict the principles established in this chapter.

---

## Article 2. Principle of Governance

### 2.1

Engineering governance SHALL be principle-driven rather than implementation-driven.

### 2.2

This Constitution defines principles, responsibilities, authorities, and constraints.

It SHALL NOT define implementation details unless explicitly required by constitutional amendment.

### 2.3

Implementation decisions belong to lower governance layers.

Lower governance layers SHALL remain consistent with this Constitution.

---

## Article 3. Principle of Separation of Concerns

### 3.1

Every engineering artifact SHALL have a clearly defined responsibility.

### 3.2

No artifact SHOULD perform multiple unrelated responsibilities.

### 3.3

Business concerns, architectural concerns, implementation concerns, operational concerns, and governance concerns SHALL remain logically separated.

### 3.4

A change in one concern SHOULD minimize its impact on unrelated concerns.

---

## Article 4. Principle of Separation of Decision and Implementation

### 4.1

Engineering decisions SHALL be documented independently from their implementations.

### 4.2

Decision artifacts describe **what** the system intends to achieve and **why** those decisions exist.

### 4.3

Implementation artifacts describe **how** those decisions are realized.

### 4.4

Implementation SHALL conform to documented decisions.

Decision documents SHALL NOT be modified to accommodate implementation constraints or shortcuts.

### 4.5

Replacement of implementation technologies SHALL NOT require modification of decision artifacts unless the underlying engineering decision itself changes.

---

## Article 5. Principle of Single Responsibility for Information

### 5.1

Every information domain SHALL have one authoritative definition.

### 5.2

Multiple independent definitions of the same information domain are prohibited.

### 5.3

Derived information SHALL reference or originate from its authoritative definition.

### 5.4

Information ownership SHALL always be identifiable.

---

## Article 6. Principle of Single Source of Truth

### 6.1

Single Source of Truth (SSOT) is an attribute of an information domain, not of a repository, directory, document, or technology.

### 6.2

Each information domain SHALL define one authoritative source.

Examples include, but are not limited to:

* Permission Definitions
* Role Definitions
* Status Definitions
* Error Code Definitions
* Validation Rules
* Design Tokens

### 6.3

Different information domains MAY have different authoritative sources.

There is no requirement that all authoritative sources reside within the same directory or document.

### 6.4

No engineering artifact may redefine information already governed by another authoritative source.

---

## Article 7. Principle of Traceability

### 7.1

Every implementation SHALL be traceable to its governing documentation.

### 7.2

Every engineering decision SHOULD be traceable to its corresponding business requirement.

### 7.3

Every released feature SHOULD be traceable through the following hierarchy:

Business Need

→ Product Requirement

→ Architecture

→ Design

→ Implementation

→ Testing

→ Release

### 7.4

Untraceable implementation SHOULD be treated as engineering debt.

---

## Article 8. Principle of Consistency

### 8.1

Consistency SHALL take precedence over individual preference.

### 8.2

Engineering standards SHALL be applied uniformly throughout the repository.

### 8.3

When multiple valid engineering approaches exist, the approach most consistent with the existing system SHOULD be preferred unless a formal architectural decision states otherwise.

---

## Article 9. Principle of Evolution

### 9.1

The repository is expected to evolve continuously.

### 9.2

Evolution SHALL preserve architectural integrity.

### 9.3

Backward compatibility SHOULD be maintained whenever reasonably practical.

### 9.4

Breaking changes SHALL follow the governance process defined by this Constitution.

---

## Article 10. Principle of Long-Term Sustainability

### 10.1

Engineering decisions SHALL prioritize long-term maintainability over short-term convenience.

### 10.2

Temporary solutions SHALL be explicitly identified.

### 10.3

Technical debt SHALL be documented.

### 10.4

Engineering quality SHALL not be intentionally compromised for development speed without explicit justification and approval.

---

## Article 11. Principle of Automation

### 11.1

Repetitive engineering activities SHOULD be automated whenever practical.

### 11.2

Automation SHALL enforce engineering standards rather than replace engineering judgment.

### 11.3

Engineering governance SHOULD rely on automated validation wherever feasible.

---

## Article 12. Principle of Technology Independence

### 12.1

This Constitution SHALL remain independent of specific programming languages, frameworks, cloud providers, databases, or development tools.

### 12.2

Technology selections belong to Architecture Governance.

### 12.3

Technology changes SHALL NOT require modification of this Constitution unless constitutional principles themselves are affected.

---

## Article 13. Principle of Continuous Improvement

### 13.1

Engineering practices SHOULD improve continuously.

### 13.2

Improvement SHALL preserve constitutional consistency.

### 13.3

No improvement may violate higher-order engineering principles established by this Constitution.

---

## End of Chapter I

# Chapter II. Governance Framework

## Article 1. Purpose

### 1.1

This chapter establishes the governance framework of the repository.

### 1.2

The governance framework defines the authority, priority, relationship, and responsibilities of all governing artifacts.

### 1.3

Every engineering artifact SHALL derive its authority from this governance framework.

---

## Article 2. Governance Hierarchy

### 2.1

Repository governance SHALL follow a hierarchical model.

Higher-level governance artifacts define constraints for lower-level artifacts.

Lower-level artifacts SHALL NOT contradict higher-level artifacts.

### 2.2

The governance hierarchy is defined as follows, from highest to lowest authority:

1. Engineering Constitution
2. Business Rules
3. Product Documentation
4. Business Documentation
5. Architecture Documentation
6. Module PRD
7. Technical Specifications
8. Source Code
9. Runtime Configuration
10. Generated Artifacts

### 2.3

Lower-level artifacts MAY further specialize higher-level rules.

They SHALL NOT redefine or invalidate higher-level principles.

---

## Article 3. Authority

### 3.1

Every engineering decision SHALL have an identifiable governing authority.

### 3.2

Authority SHALL belong to documents rather than individuals.

### 3.3

Individuals are responsible for maintaining governing artifacts.

They do not become the source of governance themselves.

### 3.4

Engineering decisions SHALL be justified by referencing applicable governing artifacts whenever possible.

---

## Article 4. Responsibility

### 4.1

Every governing artifact SHALL define its own scope and responsibility.

### 4.2

Responsibilities SHALL NOT overlap without explicit definition.

### 4.3

When responsibility overlaps are unavoidable, precedence SHALL follow the governance hierarchy.

---

## Article 5. Delegation

### 5.1

Higher-level governance MAY delegate implementation authority to lower-level artifacts.

### 5.2

Delegation transfers decision-making responsibility within a defined scope.

It SHALL NOT transfer constitutional authority.

### 5.3

Delegated authority SHALL remain bounded by the constraints imposed by higher-level governance.

---

## Article 6. Conflict Resolution

### 6.1

When multiple governing artifacts conflict, the artifact with higher authority SHALL prevail.

### 6.2

Conflicts between artifacts of equal authority SHALL be resolved through formal revision rather than individual interpretation.

### 6.3

Source code SHALL never be used to justify contradictions against governing documents.

### 6.4

Implementation convenience SHALL NOT override established governance.

---

## Article 7. Governance Compliance

### 7.1

All engineering artifacts SHALL comply with their governing artifacts.

### 7.2

Compliance SHOULD be verified through documentation review, code review, automated validation, or other appropriate governance mechanisms.

### 7.3

Failure to comply SHALL be treated as a governance issue rather than an implementation issue.

---

## Article 8. Governance Independence

### 8.1

This governance framework SHALL remain independent of organizational structure.

### 8.2

Changes in team size, project ownership, or contributor roles SHALL NOT affect the validity of this governance framework.

### 8.3

Governance SHALL outlive individual contributors.

Repository knowledge MUST remain institutional rather than personal.

---

## Article 9. Interpretation Authority

### 9.1

Interpretation of governing artifacts SHALL prioritize constitutional intent over literal wording.

### 9.2

When ambiguity exists, interpretation SHALL preserve:

* Architectural consistency
* Information integrity
* Repository maintainability
* Long-term sustainability

### 9.3

Interpretation SHALL NOT introduce new rules that have not been formally established.

---

## Article 10. Governance Evolution

### 10.1

The governance framework MAY evolve over time.

### 10.2

Governance evolution SHALL preserve backward consistency whenever practical.

### 10.3

Structural changes to governance SHALL follow the Constitutional Amendment process defined in Chapter XII.

---

## End of Chapter II

# Chapter III. Information Governance

## Article 1. Purpose

### 1.1

This chapter establishes the governance principles for all information maintained within the repository.

### 1.2

Information governance SHALL ensure that information remains accurate, consistent, traceable, and maintainable throughout its lifecycle.

### 1.3

The governance defined herein applies regardless of the physical representation of information.

---

## Article 2. Information Classification

### 2.1

All repository information SHALL belong to a clearly defined information domain.

### 2.2

Information domains MAY include, but are not limited to:

* Business Information
* Product Information
* Architecture Information
* Database Information
* API Information
* User Interface Information
* Permission Information
* Configuration Information
* Operational Information

### 2.3

Each information domain SHALL define its own ownership, lifecycle, and authoritative source.

---

## Article 3. Information Ownership

### 3.1

Every information domain SHALL have exactly one authoritative owner.

### 3.2

Ownership defines responsibility for maintaining information accuracy.

### 3.3

Ownership SHALL NOT imply exclusive implementation responsibility.

### 3.4

Changes affecting an information domain SHALL be performed through its authoritative owner.

---

## Article 4. Single Source of Truth

### 4.1

Single Source of Truth (SSOT) is a property of an information domain.

### 4.2

Every information domain SHALL define exactly one authoritative source.

### 4.3

The authoritative source MAY be:

* A governing document
* A machine-readable specification
* A database schema
* Source code
* Configuration
* Any other officially designated artifact

### 4.4

The physical location of an artifact SHALL NOT determine whether it is the Single Source of Truth.

### 4.5

No information domain SHALL have multiple independent authoritative sources.

---

## Article 5. Machine-readable Specifications

### 5.1

Machine-readable specifications SHALL exist only for information that benefits from cross-component consistency or automation.

### 5.2

Machine-readable specifications SHALL complement governance documentation.

They SHALL NOT replace business documentation, architectural documentation, or engineering decisions.

### 5.3

Machine-readable specifications MAY be referenced by multiple implementation components.

### 5.4

Automation MAY consume machine-readable specifications to generate or validate implementation artifacts.

Such automation SHALL NOT alter the governance hierarchy.

---

## Article 6. Information Derivation

### 6.1

Information MAY be derived from an authoritative source.

### 6.2

Derived information SHALL remain consistent with its authoritative source.

### 6.3

Derived information SHALL NOT redefine authoritative information.

### 6.4

Whenever practical, derivation SHOULD be automated.

### 6.5

Manual synchronization SHALL be minimized.

---

## Article 7. Information Consistency

### 7.1

Equivalent information SHALL remain semantically consistent across all artifacts.

### 7.2

Different representations of the same information SHALL NOT introduce contradictory meanings.

### 7.3

Consistency SHALL be evaluated based on semantics rather than formatting.

### 7.4

Temporary inconsistency during active development SHOULD be minimized and resolved before integration.

---

## Article 8. Information Traceability

### 8.1

Information SHALL remain traceable to its authoritative source.

### 8.2

Derived information SHOULD explicitly reference its authoritative origin whenever practical.

### 8.3

Information lineage SHOULD remain identifiable throughout the repository lifecycle.

---

## Article 9. Information Evolution

### 9.1

Information MAY evolve over time.

### 9.2

Information evolution SHALL preserve consistency across dependent artifacts.

### 9.3

Breaking information changes SHALL be communicated through the appropriate governance process.

---

## Article 10. Information Integrity

### 10.1

Repository information SHALL prioritize correctness over convenience.

### 10.2

No implementation artifact SHALL intentionally diverge from its authoritative information source.

### 10.3

Information integrity SHALL take precedence over implementation optimization.

---

## End of Chapter III

This chapter establishes the governance principles for organizing, maintaining, and managing repository artifacts.

### 1.2

Artifact categories MAY include, but are not limited to:

* Documentation
* Source Code
* Database Objects
* Deployment Assets
* Configuration
* Machine-readable Specifications
* Templates
* Static Assets
* Generated Artifacts

Artifact classification SHALL be determined by its primary responsibility rather than its file format.

### 2.4

File extensions SHALL NOT determine artifact classification.

---

## Article 3. Artifact Responsibility

### 3.1

Every artifact SHALL have a clearly defined responsibility.

### 3.2

An artifact SHOULD focus on a single primary responsibility.

### 3.3

Responsibilities SHALL NOT overlap without explicit justification.

### 3.4

Artifacts SHALL NOT become repositories of unrelated concerns.

---

## Article 4. Artifact Relationship

### 4.1

Artifacts MAY reference one another.

### 4.2

References SHALL preserve traceability.

### 4.3

References SHALL NOT introduce conflicting definitions.

### 4.4

Relationships between artifacts SHALL remain directional whenever practical.

### 4.5

Circular dependencies between artifacts SHOULD be avoided.

---

## Article 5. Repository Organization

### 5.1

Repository organization SHALL prioritize clarity over personal preference.

### 5.2

Directory structures SHALL reflect engineering responsibilities.

### 5.3

Directories SHALL group related artifacts rather than implementation technologies whenever practical.

### 5.4

Repository organization SHALL support long-term scalability.

---

## Article 6. Artifact Ownership

### 6.1

Every artifact SHALL have an identifiable owner or owning component.

### 6.2

Ownership defines maintenance responsibility.

### 6.3

Ownership SHALL NOT alter governance authority as defined in Chapter II.

---

## Article 7. Artifact Lifecycle

### 7.1

Every artifact SHALL have a defined lifecycle.

### 7.2

Artifact states MAY include:

* Draft
* Active
* Deprecated
* Archived
* Removed

### 7.3

Deprecated artifacts SHOULD remain traceable until officially removed.

### 7.4

Artifact removal SHALL preserve repository integrity.

---

## Article 8. Artifact Consistency

### 8.1

Artifacts representing related engineering concepts SHALL remain structurally consistent.

### 8.2

Naming conventions, directory conventions, and repository organization SHALL remain consistent throughout the repository.

### 8.3

Structural consistency SHALL take precedence over contributor preference.

---

## Article 9. Generated Artifacts

### 9.1

Generated artifacts SHALL be clearly distinguishable from manually maintained artifacts.

### 9.2

Generated artifacts SHALL NOT become authoritative sources unless explicitly designated.

### 9.3

Generated artifacts MAY be regenerated at any time.

### 9.4

Manual modification of generated artifacts SHOULD be avoided unless explicitly permitted.

---

## Article 10. Repository Evolution

### 10.1

Repository organization MAY evolve over time.

### 10.2

Repository restructuring SHALL preserve traceability whenever practical.

### 10.3

Repository evolution SHALL minimize disruption to existing engineering workflows.

### 10.4

Repository restructuring SHALL comply with the governance hierarchy defined in Chapter II.

This chapter establishes the governance principles for engineering documentation.

### 1.2

Documentation SHALL communicate engineering knowledge in a consistent, traceable, and maintainable manner.

### 5.1

### 5.2

Conflicting definitions across documentation are prohibited.

### 5.3

Documentation SHALL use standardized terminology whenever applicable.

### 5.4

Terminology SHALL follow the project glossary.

---

## Article 6. Documentation Traceability

### 6.1

### 6.2

Traceability SHALL support navigation across the documentation hierarchy.

### 6.3

---

## Article 7. Documentation Ownership

### 7.1

Every document SHALL have a clearly identifiable owner.

### 7.3

Ownership SHALL NOT modify document authority as defined by the governance hierarchy.

---

## Article 8. Documentation Quality

### 8.1

Documentation SHALL prioritize clarity over completeness.

### 8.2

Documentation SHALL avoid ambiguity whenever practical.

### 8.3

Documentation SHALL remain understandable without requiring knowledge of implementation details unless explicitly intended.

### 8.4

Examples MAY supplement documentation but SHALL NOT replace normative definitions.

---

## Article 9. Documentation Synchronization

### 9.1

Documentation SHALL remain synchronized with the engineering decisions it represents.

### 9.2

Implementation changes affecting documented behavior SHALL trigger documentation review.

### 9.3

Documentation SHALL NOT intentionally describe obsolete system behavior.

### 9.4

---

## Article 10. Documentation Evolution

### 10.1

Documentation MAY evolve continuously.

### 10.2

Documentation updates SHALL preserve historical traceability whenever practical.

### 10.3

Documentation restructuring SHALL preserve consistency with repository governance.

### 10.4

Documentation SHALL remain aligned with this Constitution throughout its lifecycle.

# Chapter VI. Architecture Governance

## Article 1. Purpose

### 1.1

This chapter establishes the governance principles for system architecture.

### 1.2

Architecture governance SHALL ensure that engineering decisions remain consistent, sustainable, and aligned with the constitutional principles.

### 1.3

Architecture governance regulates architectural decisions rather than implementation techniques.

---

## Article 2. Architectural Principles

### 2.1

System architecture SHALL be intentional.

Architectural decisions SHALL be made deliberately rather than emerge accidentally.

### 2.2

Architecture SHALL prioritize long-term maintainability over short-term implementation convenience.

### 2.3

Architectural consistency SHALL take precedence over isolated technical optimization.

### 2.4

Architectural complexity SHALL be justified by business value or engineering necessity.

### 2.5

Every architectural decision SHOULD support scalability, maintainability, observability, and extensibility whenever practical.

---

## Article 3. Technology Governance

### 3.1

Technology selections SHALL be governed rather than individually decided.

### 3.2

Core technologies SHALL remain consistent throughout the repository unless formally changed.

### 3.3

Technology diversity SHALL be minimized unless justified by architectural requirements.

### 3.4

Technology selection SHALL consider, at minimum:

* Functional suitability
* Long-term maintainability
* Ecosystem maturity
* Security
* Operational complexity
* Team sustainability

### 3.5

Personal preference SHALL NOT constitute sufficient justification for technology selection.

---

## Article 4. Decision Authority

### 4.1

Architectural decisions SHALL be documented.

### 4.2

Every architectural decision SHALL identify:

* Decision scope
* Decision rationale
* Expected impact
* Applicable constraints

### 4.3

Architectural decisions SHALL remain traceable throughout the engineering lifecycle.

### 4.4

Implementation SHALL comply with approved architectural decisions.

---

## Article 5. Approved Technology Process

### 5.1

Core technologies SHALL belong to the Approved Technology Stack.

### 5.2

The Approved Technology Stack SHALL be governed by architecture documentation rather than this Constitution.

### 5.3

Introduction of new core technologies SHALL follow the established architecture governance process.

### 5.4

Technology replacement SHALL include evaluation of architectural impact.

### 5.5

Experimental technologies MAY be introduced only within explicitly defined boundaries.

Experimental adoption SHALL NOT establish architectural precedent.

---

## Article 6. Architectural Consistency

### 6.1

Architectural patterns SHALL be applied consistently across the repository.

### 6.2

Equivalent architectural problems SHOULD be solved using consistent architectural approaches whenever practical.

### 6.3

Architectural exceptions SHALL be explicitly documented.

### 6.4

Undocumented architectural exceptions SHOULD be treated as architectural debt.

---

## Article 7. Architectural Evolution

### 7.1

Architecture MAY evolve continuously.

### 7.2

Architectural evolution SHALL preserve constitutional consistency.

### 7.3

Breaking architectural changes SHALL be formally reviewed before implementation.

### 7.4

Architectural evolution SHOULD minimize unnecessary disruption to existing components.

---

## Article 8. Cross-System Consistency

### 8.1

Frontend, Backend, Database, Deployment, and Supporting Tools SHALL be treated as parts of a unified engineering system.

### 8.2

Architectural decisions SHOULD consider cross-component impact rather than isolated optimization.

### 8.3

No architectural decision SHALL intentionally degrade overall system integrity for localized benefit.

---

## Article 9. Architectural Documentation

### 9.1

Architecture SHALL be documented using standardized engineering documentation.

### 9.2

Architecture documentation SHALL distinguish between:

* Principles
* Decisions
* Designs
* Constraints

### 9.3

Architecture documentation SHALL remain synchronized with the current architectural state.

### 9.4

Historical architectural decisions SHALL remain traceable.

---

## Article 10. Constitutional Compliance

### 10.1

Architecture governance SHALL derive its authority from this Constitution.

### 10.2

No architectural decision SHALL override constitutional principles.

### 10.3

Conflicts between architectural decisions and constitutional principles SHALL be resolved by constitutional precedence.

---

## End of Chapter VI

Engineering governance SHALL be organized into hierarchical levels of authority.

| Level   | Governance Artifact                 | Purpose                                                      |
| ------- | ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |

            │
            ▼

            │
            ▼

            │
            ▼
Implementation Artifacts

Lower-level artifacts SHALL implement, support, or derive from higher-level artifacts.

Higher-level artifacts SHALL NOT be rewritten by lower-level artifacts.

Implementation SHALL NOT redefine governing decisions.
